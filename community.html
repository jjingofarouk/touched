Empowering Communities

Touched Hearts builds sustainable, resilient communities across Uganda through holistic development initiatives that address fundamental needs while preserving local culture and promoting self-reliance.

Our Community Development Approach

Since 2010, Touched Hearts has partnered with over 35 communities across Uganda to create lasting positive change. We believe that true development comes from within communities themselves, which is why our programs are designed with and led by local leaders and residents.

Our community development work focuses on addressing fundamental needs—clean water, food security, economic empowerment, and infrastructure—while strengthening social cohesion and preserving cultural heritage. We employ a participatory approach that ensures community ownership and sustainability long after our direct involvement ends.

Key Community Initiatives

What We’ve Done So Far

▼

We’ve already made a quiet mark in these villages. Over the past year, we handed out sanitary pads to 300 girls in Kabale District, giving them a chance to stay in school without worry. It’s a small thing, but their smiles say it’s big.

We also fixed up a rusty spring with local help to bring clean water to 100 people who used to walk miles. And in Masaka District, we gave 20 women a little seed money and tools to start weaving mats together—now they sell them at the market.

Clean Water for Families

▼

We dream of a day when no one here hauls water over long, dusty paths. By 2026, we hope to install 1 borehole and patch up a few springs in Kisoro District. Picture a simple gravity-flow system flowing clean water to 300 homes—folks who’ve never had it so close.

We’ll train a handful of villagers to keep it running, who will also collect a few coins for repairs and teach kids to wash their hands. If it works, we could cut sickness in this area nearly in half.

Helping Farmers Grow

▼

We want to see fields bloom even when the rains play tricks. Soon, we’ll bring 200 farmers simple tricks—like tougher crops that fight drought. In Karamoja, where we hope to implement this first, we hope their harvests could grow by 20% to give families a little more to eat or sell.

We’ll set up 3 small demo plots to show off composting and terracing, plus start a tiny seed-swapping circle. It won’t be much, but it’ll be a start—something they can carry forward.

Women Supporting Women

▼

Imagine women gathering under a tree, dreaming bigger. We’ll grow the Tusubira Women’s Circle in Kisoro District from 25 to 40 initially, to help them sell baskets and soap. We hope to get 50 women small loans for their ideas to lift them up.

More than cash, we want them to find their voices and speak up in village talks. It’s a long shot, but if they keep paying back like they do now, we believe it’s a future worth chasing.

Building Community Spaces

▼

We’re aiming to build 4 basic shelters—mud walls, tin roofs—places for meetings or kids’ homework. In certain districts like Bundibugyo, one could keep a few more students in school. During floods, these will be a dry spot for them to rest.

We also hope to fix the wobbly bridge in Kitebero, Mbarara District and clear 5 kilometers of muddy paths so that working women can reach the market easier.

Caring for the Land

▼

We see a greener tomorrow with 1,000 trees taking root, guarded by villagers who care. We will hand out 100 clay stoves to cut firewood use and clear the air in smoky kitchens.

By Lake Victoria, we will teach 50 fishermen to fish smarter and set aside a small water patch for fish to grow.

Community Transformation Stories

Namayingo Village - Water Access

Until 2022, residents of Namayingo village collected water from a contaminated stream, resulting in frequent cholera outbreaks. Our borehole project, maintained by a local committee, has eliminated waterborne diseases and given women and children back four hours daily previously spent collecting water.

Image: [Namayingo water point inauguration]

Tusubira Women's Group - Masaka

What began as a small savings group has transformed into a thriving cooperative that produces organic shea butter products sold throughout Uganda. Members now earn three times their previous income, and many have built permanent homes and sent their children to secondary school.

Image: [Women making shea products]

Katwe Youth Association

In this urban slum area, unemployment and crime rates were high until our skills training program equipped 150 young people with electrical, plumbing, and construction skills. Today, 85% are employed or running their own businesses, and crime rates have dropped by 40%.

Image: [Youth in skills training]
